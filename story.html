OUR HERITAGE

About Us

We are a design-led brand rooted in Dhokra, one of India’s oldest metal casting traditions. Working closely with artisan communities, we reinterpret this ancient technique into objects that bridge past and present.

Each piece is entirely handcrafted, bearing the subtle variations that make it unique—pièces uniques shaped by hand. The irregularities are not imperfections, but signatures of time, process, and the human hand, an expression of true savoir-faire.

Our approach is guided by balance, honouring tradition while embracing contemporary design. Through considered forms and restrained aesthetics, we create objects that are tactile, enduring, and quietly distinctive—objets d’art designed to live effortlessly within modern interiors.

At our core, we are not simply preserving a craft; we are contributing to its continuity. By placing Dhokra within a global, contemporary context, we aim to build a dialogue between past and present, between artisan and collector.

Each creation is shaped slowly and intentionally. It carries not only form, but memory of material, of process, of the hands that made it.

We stand for meaningful design rooted in craftsmanship, quality, and cultural integrity. Committed to responsible production, we work in close collaboration with artisans, supporting sustainable practices and fair livelihoods, an approach grounded in artisanat responsable.

Our pieces are created for those who value authenticity—objects with presence, purpose, and a story to live with. In each work, we invite you to experience l’art de vivre: to embrace craftsmanship, cherish tradition, and carry beauty into everyday life.

History of Dhokra

Dhokra is one of India’s oldest surviving metal casting traditions, with origins that trace back over 4,000 years. Its earliest expression is often associated with the iconic Dancing Girl of the Indus Valley Civilisation, a small bronze figure that reflects the same lost-wax casting technique still practised by Dhokra artisans today.

At the heart of Dhokra lies this ancient process, known as the lost-wax method. Each piece begins as a core of clay, shaped by hand and layered with beeswax threads to create intricate patterns. This wax model is then encased in another layer of clay and fired in a kiln. As the wax melts away, it leaves behind a cavity into which molten metal, typically brass, is poured. Once cooled, the outer mould is broken to reveal the final form. Because the mould is destroyed in the process, no two pieces can ever be the same.

Historically, Dhokra has been practised by nomadic artisan communities, often referred to as the Dhokra Damar tribes, who moved across regions of eastern and central India. Today, clusters of artisans continue this tradition in states such as West Bengal, Odisha, Chhattisgarh, and Jharkhand. Their work was traditionally tied to ritual and everyday life, creating objects such as deities, tribal figures, ornaments, and functional vessels.

What makes Dhokra distinct is its raw, expressive quality. The visible textures, the linear patterns formed by wax threads, and the organic irregularities all speak of a process that is deeply manual and time intensive. It is a craft shaped not by precision tools, but by memory, skill, and repetition passed down through generations.

Over time, Dhokra has evolved from being primarily ritualistic and utilitarian to finding a place in contemporary design and global markets. Yet, its essence remains unchanged as it is still made slowly, entirely by hand, and in direct dialogue with material and maker.

Today, Dhokra stands as more than a craft; it is a living tradition. It carries within it histories of migration, community, and adaptation, continuing to evolve while holding on to the integrity of its origins.

PROCESS

The Art of Lost Wax Casting

01
The Core Sculpting

The process begins with gathering clay from local riverbeds. The artisan mixes this clay with rice husk and shapes it into the basic core form of the desired object. This core is then left to dry in the sun.

02
Wax Threading

Pure beeswax is mixed with resin and nut oil, then extruded through a traditional press to create long, hair-thin threads. The artisan meticulously wraps these threads around the dried clay core, creating the intricate designs, motifs, and textures that define Dhokra art.

03
The Outer Mold

Once the wax design is complete, it is covered with another layer of fine clay, followed by a thicker layer of clay mixed with cow dung and husk. A small channel (runner) is left open at the top. The entire piece is dried again.

04
The "Lost Wax" Firing

The clay mold is baked in a traditional wood-fired furnace. As the temperature rises, the wax inside melts and drains out through the channel—hence the term "lost wax" (cire perdue). This leaves a perfect hollow cavity inside the baked clay mold, bearing the exact negative impression of the wax design.

05
Metal Casting

Scrap brass, bronze, and copper are melted in a crucible at temperatures exceeding 1000°C. The glowing, molten metal is carefully poured into the channel of the hot clay mold, filling the void left by the melted wax.

06
The Reveal & Finishing

After cooling, the outer clay shell is broken open to reveal the solidified metal casting. Because the mold must be destroyed, every single Dhokra piece is a unique, unrepeatable original. The piece is then cleaned, polished, and given an antique patina finish.
